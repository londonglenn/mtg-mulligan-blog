Learning Mulligan Decisions in Magic: The Gathering

Modeling one of the most important early-game decisions using data

Tool Repo Model Repo

Overview

Mulligan decisions are one of the highest-impact choices in Magic: The Gathering. Given only your opening hand, you must decide whether to keep or redraw— trading immediate playability against long-term resources.

In this project, I model this decision as a probability:

P(Keep | Hand)

Using real player data, I train models to predict this decision and analyze what actually matters.

Key result: Most of the decision is driven by mana (land count), not card complexity.

The Decision Problem

A mulligan decision is fundamentally a tradeoff:

  • Keep → risk a weak hand
  • Mulligan → lose a card

Ideally, you would compare:

E[Win | Keep, S] vs E[Win | Mulligan, S]

But this is impossible to compute due to randomness, hidden information, and the massive number of possible game outcomes.

Why This Is Hard

  • Partial observability (you don’t know future draws)
  • Huge combinatorial space of hands
  • Complex interactions between cards

As a result, players rely on heuristics like “2–3 lands is good”— but these rules are informal and hard to analyze.

Approach

Instead of solving the full game, we learn directly from player behavior:

P(Keep | S)

where S represents the opening hand and context.

Decision Rule

Keep if P(Keep | S) > τ

Learning Setup

  • Binary classification (Keep vs Mulligan)
  • Trained using log loss (probability calibration)
  • 5-fold cross-validation

Data Collection

There is no public dataset for mulligan decisions, so I built a custom tool.

Players are shown random hands and choose keep or mulligan. This converts human intuition into labeled training data.

Feature Design

  • Land count: captures mana availability
  • Card identity: which cards appear
  • Mana curve: distribution of costs
  • Play/Draw: context matters

Design assumption: decisions are driven primarily by mana consistency

Decision ≈ f(mana) + g(cards)

Model Performance

All models achieve strong performance (ROC-AUC > 0.90). Surprisingly, simple logistic regression performs nearly as well as more complex models.

This suggests that feature design matters more than model complexity.

What Actually Matters?

Removing land features significantly hurts performance, while removing card features has a smaller effect.

Land count alone explains most of the decision.

Interpreting the Model

The model learns a nonlinear relationship:

  • 2–3 lands → highest keep probability
  • Too few or too many → bad

This matches real player intuition—but now we can quantify it.

Key Insight

Mulligan decisions are mostly a resource problem, not a card problem.

We can decompose the decision:

π(S) = π_land(S) + δ_card(S)

Land consistency determines the baseline, and cards make small adjustments.

Interactive Demo

The demo shows the model evaluating real hands and outputting a keep probability. This allows players to compare their intuition against the model.

Limitations

  • Limited dataset size
  • No deck archetype modeling
  • Reflects player behavior, not optimal play

Future Work

  • Incorporate deck archetypes
  • Model card synergy explicitly
  • Predict win probability directly
  • Scale data collection

Conclusion

Despite the complexity of Magic, mulligan decisions follow a simple structure: they are dominated by mana consistency.

This suggests that even complex strategic decisions can often be approximated with interpretable, low-dimensional models.
